Ms. Anjana Vijayan, a PhD scholar in the Department of Chemistry, will be

*Title:* Mechanistic Exploration of Gas-Phase Chemiluminescence using
Multi-Reference Electronic Structure Methods
*Date:* 27th January 2025
*Time:* 11:30 AM
*Venue:* A2, 119

*Abstract: *Chemiluminescence (CL) involves the emission of light as a
result of a chemical reaction. Such a process occurring inside a living
organism is referred to as bioluminescence, with examples including firefly
beetle, dinoflagellates and some fungi. In most of the CL systems, light
emission originates from the dissociation of a certain cyclic peroxide
ring, where conical intersections near the ground-state transition state
enable the molecule to populate multiple electronic states. However, CL
systems other than cyclic peroxides remain underexplored, and a general
mechanistic framework of cold-light emission is still lacking. A recent
theoretical study on air afterglow (NO+O) CL reaction revealed a distinct
mechanism in which the ground-state pathway bifurcates via valley-ridge
inflection, leading to the formation of NO2 in the ground and excited
electronic states. Motivated from these insights, we are currently
exploring the mechanistic details of SO+O reaction using multireference
electronic structure methods such as CASSCF and CASPT2, combined with
non-adiabatic dynamics. The study involves constructing potential energy
surfaces (PESs), identifying minimum energy paths, and computing branching
ratios among the accessible reaction channels. Our results suggest that the
formation of SO2 in different electronic states, interestingly, involves a
ridge-mediated bifurcation of the first triplet-state pathway. SO2
afterglow, has been primarily attributed to the triplet states of sulfur
dioxide (SO2) along with singlet excited states. CL mechanism in SO+O
aligns more closely with the (NO+O) CL reaction.This study provides new
fundamental insights into the chemiluminescence phenomenon and also gives
evidence that VRI-mediated pathways can be general motifs in gas-phase CL
reactions.
